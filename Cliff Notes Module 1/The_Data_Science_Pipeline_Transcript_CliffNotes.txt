The Data Science Pipeline — Study Guide
Course: Introduction to Data-Centric Computing (CMPINF-2100)
Source: The Data Science Pipeline lecture transcript

================================================================
CORE CONCEPT
================================================================
The "data science pipeline" is the process you'll follow throughout
this course and the MDS program. When deep in technical weeds,
"pop up a level" and identify which pipeline step you're on — it
keeps purpose in view.

Underlying framework: CRISP-DM (Cross-Industry Standard Process for
Data Mining). Widely used in industry and research. Worth looking up
independently; you'll encounter it repeatedly.

================================================================
THE SIX STEPS
================================================================

STEP 1 — RESEARCH QUESTION / BUSINESS OBJECTIVE
  Always start here. You want to know SOMETHING from data.
  Examples given:
    - Early indicators of developing diabetes?
    - What type of customers is my business losing?
    - Where will housing prices rise or fall?
    - What video will go viral?
  Must be answerable with quantitative measures.

STEP 2 — SELECT AND UNDERSTAND DATA SETS
  Identify which data sets can help answer the question.
  - Sometimes good data doesn't exist; you may have to find or
    derive it.
  - In later MDS courses you'll source your own data; in THIS
    course you'll work with pre-collected sets.
  - Always ask: Why was this data collected? How was it collected?
    How representative is it of the phenomenon under study?

  Instructor's social media bot example: different "bot data sets"
  vary wildly by collection method. Some find a seed of bots and
  expand by similarity — but if the seed is all political bots,
  the data set misses news bots and other types entirely. Lesson:
  scrutinize collection method and look for variety. Ask what
  might be MISSING from the data.

STEP 3 — PREPARE AND CLEAN THE DATA
  Often cited as ~80% of a data scientist's work.

  Why so much:
    - Data is often incomplete → handle missing values
    - Irrelevant data → remove what doesn't fit the question
    - Wrong granularity → aggregate (e.g., cities → states/regions),
      potentially CREATING new attributes
    - Multiple sources → combining and integrating data sets
    - Type/format issues — numbers stored as strings, inconsistent
      abbreviations, weird formatting — must be normalized

STEP 4 — EXPLORE THE DATA
  Identify and visualize basic trends, counts, distributions.
  - What are the most/least frequent values?
  - How many data points exist across different attribute values?
    (Instructor's example: how many McDonald's are carrying
    shamrock shakes this year, and where?)
  - Summary statistics
  - Visible trends that bear on the research question

  CRITICAL NUANCE: Steps 3 and 4 are iterative, not strictly
  sequential. You'll clean → explore → spot an outlier → return
  to cleaning. Outliers may be mistakes to remove OR genuinely
  important signals. Judgment required.

STEP 5 — STATISTICAL MODELING
  Identify statistical patterns within data. Most often takes the
  form of PREDICTIVE MODELING.

  The structure:
    - One attribute is the OUTCOME you care about (the thing you
      want to predict)
    - Other attributes are candidate PREDICTORS
    - Test which predictors are associated with / can predict the
      outcome

  Examples mapped onto this structure:
    - Outcome: developing diabetes
      Predictors: health history attributes
    - Outcome: customer churn (stops being a customer)
      Predictors: customer attributes correlated with churn

STEP 6 — COMMUNICATE THE RESULTS
  Evaluate and interpret the models, then tell stakeholders what
  was found.

  Key constraints:
    - Stakeholders likely DO NOT have an MDS background. Translate.
    - Convey trends, associations, and predictions clearly
    - More broadly: tell a STORY — what was learned from the data,
      why it matters, and how it answers the original question

  This step closes the loop back to Step 1.

================================================================
HIGH-VALUE TAKEAWAYS FOR EXAM / APPLICATION
================================================================
  - Memorize the 6 steps in order. Be able to name CRISP-DM as
    the underlying framework.
  - The pipeline is iterative, not strictly linear (especially
    cleaning ↔ exploration).
  - 80% of the work is cleaning/preparation — internalize this
    expectation.
  - Always interrogate data provenance: who collected it, how,
    and what's missing.
  - Modeling is structured around one outcome attribute and
    multiple predictor attributes.
  - Communication step is non-optional — analysis that can't be
    explained to a stakeholder hasn't completed the pipeline.
